# Personalization Rubric

## Purpose
The Personalization rubric evaluates how deeply and naturally the user-provided information (child's name, personality, favorite things, etc.) is integrated into the story and illustrations.

## Scoring Criteria (1-5)
The Personalization score is assigned based on the following levels:

| Score | Rating | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Excellent | The story feels uniquely crafted for the child, with deep integration of their traits. (子ども本人らしい) |
| 4 | Good | User information is naturally reflected and influences the story or visuals. (自然に反映) |
| 3 | Fair | Personalization is present but feels somewhat shallow or formulaic. (やや浅い) |
| 2 | Generic | Minimal personalization, mostly limited to name replacement. (generic) |
| 1 | Poor | No reflection of user input, or information is used inappropriately. (反映なし/不適切) |

## Evaluation Axes
Reviewers should consider the following axes when determining the score:

- **Child Profile Usage**: Are the child's personality, age, and interests used to drive their actions in the story?
- **Favorite Things Integration**: Do the child's favorite toys, foods, or animals appear as central elements or meaningful details?
- **Family & Memory Context**: Is the story context appropriate for the child's family situation or specific memories provided?
- **Name/Nickname Usage**: Is the name used naturally in dialogue and narration without being excessive?
- **Naturalness**: Does the personalization feel like a part of the story, or does it feel "tacked on"?

## Examples

### Good (Score 4-5)
- **Deep Integration**: A child who loves "dinosaurs" and "soccer" is the hero of a story about a dinosaur soccer match, using their specific favorite dinosaur.
- **Visual Reflection**: If a child is described as having "red glasses," those glasses are consistently shown in all illustrations.

### Poor (Score 1-2)
- **Search-and-Replace**: The story is clearly a generic template where only the name has been swapped, with no regard for the child's age or interests.
- **Ignoring Input**: The user specified the child loves "cats," but the story and images are all about "dogs."

## Nuances
- **Over-Personalization**: Using too much personal data can sometimes make the narrative feel cluttered. The best personalization feels effortless.
- **Privacy**: While depth is good, avoid over-exposing sensitive personal details in the story text (e.g., specific home addresses or school names).
